Why the 26× ROAS is misleading

A 26× blended ROAS is a strong number. But it's a blended number — it averages together campaigns that are genuinely acquiring new customers and campaigns that are retargeting people who were already going to buy. When most spend is in retargeting, a high blended ROAS is expected regardless of whether the ads are growing the business.

New customer acquisition is down -33% YoY — the retained base is masking it

Shopify's own order data shows Q1 2026 averaged 547 new customers/month — down from 824/month in Q1 2025. That's a -33% year-over-year decline in new customer acquisition. Revenue is roughly flat (+2.9% YoY) because the retained customer base is compensating. The repeat rate (51.0% over 15 months) is genuinely strong — above the 25–30% DTC benchmark. But retention compounds only if new customers keep entering the funnel.

The channel-level attribution data reveals why. Google last-click accounts for 34.8% of revenue — but 62% of Google's attributed orders are returning customers navigating back via search (918 returning vs 552 new). Direct traffic is 69% returning customers. Both of these are loyal buyers using Google or a bookmark to find their way back — not new customer acquisition. Facebook/Meta, despite spending less, is actually the most new-customer-efficient paid channel: 51% of Meta last-click orders are from new customers. The acquisition funnel is weakening on the channels responsible for it.

The business is running on its retained base. That's not inherently bad — 51% repeat rate is strong. But without new customers entering the funnel, the retained base ages out and doesn't compound. Rebuilding new customer acquisition is the #1 structural priority.
The lens that changes everything

Split every performance conversation into two numbers: blended ROAS (what exists today) and new-customer ROAS (what drives growth). The first tells you if the account is efficient. The second tells you if the business is growing. Right now only the first is visible.

Why the ROAS on retargeting looks so strong

The DynamicRemarketing campaign reports 50.94× ROAS. The Retention campaign reports 32.21×. These numbers are real — those campaigns are genuinely converting at high rates. But the reason is attribution, not acquisition.

When someone visits the site organically, adds to cart, leaves, and then clicks a retargeting ad two days later — that purchase is attributed 100% to the ad. But that person was likely going to buy. The ad may have accelerated the purchase by a day or two, but the demand existed before the ad was served.

This is the structural reason blended ROAS can look strong while the business doesn't grow: you can run a profitable retargeting programme forever without acquiring a single net-new customer.

Why pausing spend makes ROAS look better — and why that's the trap

A Marketing Mix Model (MMM) built on 15 months of Shopify and Meta data reveals something important: a large portion of weekly revenue is organic and baseline-driven. This was confirmed empirically during April–October 2024, when ad spend was significantly lower and the business still produced approximately $200K/month in revenue. That demand exists independently of paid media.

Meta adstock decays in 1–2 weeks.

The contribution of Meta ads to revenue fades within two weeks of any pause. When spend is paused, the organic baseline continues — and with no ad spend to separate it from, the whole number looks like ROAS improving. This creates a behavioral trap.

The trap in sequence:
Spend is paused → organic demand continues → ROAS appears to spike → this looks like confirmation that the ads weren't working → budget stays reduced → the prospecting pipeline that would create new demand never gets built → the retained base slowly ages out → revenue eventually follows.

The spike is the organic floor showing through. It is not evidence that paid media doesn't contribute — it is evidence that the contribution is short-cycle and disappears when the tap is turned off. The $200K/month organic baseline is the proof of concept that demand exists. Paid media's job is to grow the pool of people who enter that demand funnel, not to sustain what's already there.

Note on model completeness: The MMM cannot be fully specified until Google Ads data is accessible. $711K in Shopify last-click attributed to Google over 15 months — a channel equivalent in scale to Meta — is currently a black box. The channel contribution percentages in this model are directional; the structural findings (organic baseline, adstock decay) are reliable.
What we'd do

Keep retargeting — it converts well and the unit economics are sound. But cap its frequency and budget allocation to prevent further pool exhaustion. The growth question is what happens alongside it: a dedicated prospecting campaign, with cold-audience creative, tracked on new-customer ROAS separately. That's the part that doesn't exist yet.

Right now, very little budget is dedicated to reaching cold audiences — which means the retargeting pool is gradually shrinking, not growing.

Creative: what 76 ads and 3 statistical models actually show

A full analysis of 76 creatives across Forward Stepwise, Lasso, and Random Forest models reveals specific, actionable patterns — not opinions about what looks good.

The single strongest signal: persona-less ads fail.

Ads classified as targeting "general shoppers" (no specific audience identity) return 5.0× ROAS vs the account median of 19.8×. This is the highest-confidence finding across all three models — Stepwise, Lasso, and Random Forest agree. Meta's own delivery algorithm also penalises these ads with lower spend allocation. The account is producing persona-less creative at scale and the algorithm is confirming what the conversion data shows.

Marginal ROAS: no revenue ceiling reached — but attribution is blind

Marginal ROAS analysis across 16 campaigns found no campaigns have reached a strong revenue ceiling at current spend levels. There is channel headroom — with better creative, more new-customer focus, and a refreshed audience pool. However: 9 of 16 campaigns have no UTM tracking. The 2.1× Meta/GA4 attribution gap cannot be diagnosed or closed without fixing this. Adding {{campaign.name}} as utm_campaign is a 15-minute admin task that unblocks the attribution picture.

What we'd do

Immediately: Pause 58 zero-conversion ads and flat discount creative. Redirect that budget. Month 2: Launch dedicated prospecting campaigns targeting cold audiences — minimum 30% of Meta spend toward net-new customer acquisition. Alongside: Track and report new-customer ROAS separately from blended ROAS so the prospecting investment can be evaluated on its own terms.

Data quality note — GA4 and Shopify report different checkout rates

Two tools are measuring the same funnel and showing different numbers. GA4 reports ATC→Checkout at 30.77%. Shopify's native funnel reports 46.4%. Both are directional only — here's why.

The true ATC→Checkout rate is unknown — likely somewhere between 30.77% and 46.4%. The bot inflation in Shopify's session count (6.96M raw vs ~270K real sessions from GA4) is uniform across funnel steps, so dividing by 4.2x doesn't change the ratio. The discrepancy reflects a genuine measurement difference between how each tool tracks cart and checkout events.

What this means: The ATC friction is likely real — mobile UX evidence (session recordings, scroll depth, price-reveal timing) supports it regardless of the exact rate. But the $25–40K CRO recovery estimate is illustrative until bot traffic is cleaned from both sources and rates are re-measured. Priority 0: implement GA4 bot filter and remove language sitemaps before treating any funnel metric as definitive.
Multi-item basket opportunity — 2.3× LTV for buyers who buy multiple items first

Shopify cohort data across 15 months shows that customers who bought multiple items on their first order generate 2.3× the mean LTV of single-item first-order buyers.

The right move: A/B test a "complete the look" bundle prompt (jeans + matching jacket framing) on the PDP or cart page. If the test shows that the prompt lifts subsequent LTV for nudged buyers vs control, scale it. If not, the 2.3× gap is selection bias and the lever is acquisition, not on-site nudging.
Why 69% of carts never reach checkout — three fixable causes

This is a mobile UX problem, not a pricing or product problem. The evidence: Checkout→Purchase (63.9%) is healthy. Buyers who reach checkout are converting. They're not reaching checkout.

Fix 1

Price and ATC button are below the fold on mobile. A buyer on a typical phone has to scroll 600px+ before they can see the price or tap to purchase. The single most important conversion element is invisible on first load.

Fix 2

Full-width red "CLICK HERE IF YOUR SIZE IS UNAVAILABLE" banner on the Biker Jeans product page. Appears before the customer selects a size — signals inventory anxiety at the moment of decision. The fix: replace with per-size availability signals below the size grid (e.g. "Only 2 left in XL").

Fix 3

Shipping cost ($16–18) revealed for the first time at checkout. The total order cost appears only after the customer proceeds to checkout — a price-shock moment at the highest-abandonment step in the funnel.

All three are Shopify theme changes — no dev sprint, no platform work, no ad spend required.

More friction across the site — expanded page audit

Beyond the three PDP friction points, a heuristic audit covering five page types identified additional friction — all fixable without development work.

Cart

Users visit the cart 5.03× per session on average — but don't commit. The cart has no urgency signals, no low-stock indicators, no cross-sell, and no trust badges near the checkout button. With 1,400 SKUs and a loyal returning customer base, a basic "Complete the Look" or Frequently Bought Together module is a direct AOV lift with zero acquisition cost.

Homepage

No brand value proposition above the fold. Cold traffic lands on a product grid with no headline, no brand statement, and zero answer to "why Makobi?" Fresh Clean Threads shows 65K+ reviews above the fold. Makobi's homepage answers no questions about why a first-time visitor should stay.

Reviews

0–1 reviews across audited PDPs. The Biker Jeans PDP has 1 review. The Angelo Tee — a core $70–80 jersey tee — has 0 reviews. Fashion purchases at this price point require social proof for cold traffic. Competitors show 40,000+ review counts above the fold. A post-purchase email review request campaign is a one-week fix.

Collections

The main /collections/all page sorts A-to-Z by default — showing children's jackets ("B1026 Kids Bergamo") first because B-codes sort before M-codes (men's). US adult basics shoppers see kids' apparel as their first impression of the Makobi catalog. A single default sort setting change in Shopify admin fixes this in under 60 seconds.

New Arrivals

The /collections/new-arrival page has no hero or editorial framing. Meta ad traffic landing here hits a filter bar and a 2-column grid with no welcome moment, no brand context, no hook. Cold traffic from a "New Arrivals" ad receives zero reason to browse. A styled hero image with one headline would capture the investment already being made to get visitors there.

Product Names

Internal SKU codes are exposed in product names across the catalog. "Jersey Shirt | M393 Angelo Tee - Black" appears on collection grids and in browser tabs. Shoppers scanning at 1 second per card read "M393" as noise, not a brand name. Renaming storefront titles to customer-facing format is a one-time admin task.

None of the above require development, design sprints, or ad spend. They are Shopify admin changes, email automations, and theme settings — the kind of fixes that typically take 2–3 hours each to implement.

These actions together target an estimated $40–65K/month in near-term recoverable revenue from existing traffic — no new customers required. What Buck Mason is doing — and what it leaves open

Buck Mason is the only DTC basics brand with growing Share of Search. Its positioning is built on three pillars: Made in USA production, heavyweight material specs, and quality permanence ("shirts that last years, not months"). The two assets Makobi has that no digital-native competitor can match

The positioning opportunity only works if it's credible. Two assets make it credible — and neither is being used in any current advertising.

Asset 1

In-house production. Makobi manufactures domestically with direct quality control. This is the claim Buck Mason built its brand on — and Makobi can make it too. No True Classic, FCT, or BYLT equivalent exists. This asset has never appeared in an ad.

Asset 2

Big & Tall organic foothold. The Big & Tall landing page already generates 7,617 organic sessions — the top non-branded SEO page in the account. Buyers are finding Makobi through this search intent without a single ad dedicated to it. The demand is there. The identity above the fold isn't.

One important gate: The quality permanence story only works if the product is genuinely defensible. If quality is parity with True Classic, the positioning creates a Reddit liability rather than an asset. Product validation — wash tests, GSM specs, wear-cycle evidence — must precede the positioning claim at scale.
The organic search opportunity — buyers are searching, nobody is answering

The Big & Tall landing page is generating 7,617 organic sessions without a single piece of content or ad budget dedicated to it. That's not a floor — that's a proof of concept. Makobi can rank for this intent. It just hasn't tried.

Problem vocabulary — the searches buyers make after a True Classic or FCT shirt disappoints them — is rising +75.6% YoY. Terms like "non-shrink t-shirt," "heavyweight t-shirt that lasts," and "best quality basics" represent high-intent, frustration-driven queries from buyers who have already been through the low-quality cycle. They are actively searching for what Makobi makes. Right now, they are finding Buck Mason and generic Amazon results.

Google Search is the short-path version of the same bet — but the picture is more complex than it looks. Shopify's traffic data shows Google as the #1 last-click revenue channel (34.8% of revenue, $308K in 90 days). But when you split that by customer type: 918 of those orders are from returning customers using Google to navigate back — only 552 are new customers. Google's high last-click revenue is largely loyal buyers Googling the brand name, not net-new acquisition. The critical unknown: the third-party agency managing Google Ads has no audit access granted. If they're running primarily branded search, Google Ads is functioning as a retention navigation tool, not acquisition. If they're running non-branded problem vocabulary ("big and tall basics," "t-shirt that doesn't shrink"), those 522 first-click new customer orders represent genuine top-of-funnel value. Getting access to the Google Ads account isn't just a nice-to-have — it's the only way to know whether the highest-revenue channel is acquiring customers or just capturing existing demand.
Why this opportunity is structural — how buyers in this category actually decide

The competitive data shows the opportunity. The buyer research explains why it's durable — and why the same mistakes won't work here.

Reddit is the purchase gate, not just the complaint forum

High-intent basics buyers — the ones who research before buying and have high LTV — validate every new brand on Reddit before purchasing. This isn't a secondary check; it's the primary trust mechanism for the category. A brand with no Reddit presence is effectively invisible to this segment regardless of how much it spends on ads. The quality-decline threads for True Classic and FCT don't just hurt those brands — they're actively redirecting buyer attention toward whoever answers the search that follows. Right now that search lands on Buck Mason.

One failure triggers a permanent switch — there is no forgiveness cycle

When a basics shirt fails — collar bacon, transparency after one wash, shrinkage on the first cycle — the buyer doesn't complain to the brand. They post on Reddit, switch immediately, and never return. The category research is unambiguous: there is no accumulated-disappointment threshold. One visible failure ends the relationship. This is why competitor quality decline is an irreversible opportunity: those customers are not going back. They are actively in market for a replacement brand right now.

These buyers are cynical — "premium quality" is a red flag, not a selling point

Buyers in this category have been through enough brand cycles that they have developed a specific resistance to quality claims. "Premium quality," "built to last," "our best shirt yet" — these phrases have been used by every brand that later disappointed them. At this sophistication level, the claim itself triggers skepticism. The only messaging that clears this bar is mechanism transparency: not "our quality is great" but "here is specifically how we control for quality and why it doesn't degrade." In-house production is not valuable because it sounds good — it's valuable because it is the mechanism that competitors cannot credibly claim, and mechanism claims are the only ones this audience trusts.

Pack purchases are evaluated per unit — the math matters more than the price

Buyers in this category don't experience a $110 purchase as "$110 for t-shirts." They experience it as "$18.33 per shirt." The per-unit anchoring is automatic and universal across segments. The corollary: a $110 pack that lasts 3 years beats a $75 pack that fails in year one at every unit-economics calculation. This is the mechanism behind why bundle/pack-framed creative significantly outperforms single-item messaging in the account — the creative that surfaces the per-unit math is speaking the language buyers are already using internally.

The job-to-be-done is ending the search — not finding a shirt

The primary emotional driver for high-intent basics buyers is not "I want a better t-shirt." It is "I want to stop thinking about t-shirts." Every buyer who has cycled through True Classic, FCT, and BYLT is not looking for the next product — they are looking for the last one. Brands that position as a product lose to brands that position as a resolution. The creative and positioning that wins is "the basics quest is over" — not "here's why our shirt is better." The 51% 12-month repeat rate in Makobi's own Shopify data confirms this: the buyers who find the brand come back at above-benchmark rates because basics purchasing, once resolved, becomes automatic.

What we'd do

Build a prospecting creative system around two angles: "Built for your frame" (Big & Tall identity, explicitly for non-standard bodies) and "The last basics you replace" (quality permanence, backed by production story and wash-test evidence). On the organic side: optimize the existing B&T landing page above the fold, and publish 2–3 pieces of content targeting the rising problem vocabulary ("why basics shrink," "heavyweight vs standard cotton"). These compound over time and give the paid prospecting something to link to. Google Search can capture the same intent immediately while organic builds.
